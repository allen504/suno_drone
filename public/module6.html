Module 6: Transmission & Security

In the final module, students investigate how data moves between the drone and its controller, and how to protect that communication from common threats.

Main curriculum weeks: 27 (Cybersecurity Fundamentals), 28 (Wireless Threats), 29 (Threat Modeling), 30 (Implementing Countermeasures), 31 (Demonstration & Reflection)

Week 27 – Cybersecurity Fundamentals

Students are introduced to core security ideas like confidentiality, integrity, and availability, and connect them to drone systems.

Goals

  • Learn the CIA triad and basic security terminology.
  • Identify where data is stored and transmitted in their drone system.
  • Discuss what it means to “secure” a drone in practice.

In-Class Activities

  • Mini-lecture on key cybersecurity concepts.
  • Group brainstorm of ways a drone system could be attacked.
  • Map CIA concepts to different parts of their own build.

Why This Matters

Drones are networked computers with motors attached. Security thinking is essential to prevent misuse and protect people and data.

Week 28 – Wireless Threats

Students explore common wireless threats like interception, jamming, and spoofing, and consider how these could affect a drone in the real world.

Goals

  • Recognize common wireless attack types.
  • Connect each threat to potential drone failures or misuse cases.
  • Begin thinking about defenses and mitigations.

In-Class Activities

  • Discuss case studies of wireless attacks in the news (age-appropriate).
  • Identify which parts of the drone communication stack are exposed.
  • Brainstorm practical classroom-safe demonstrations of interference.

Why This Matters

Understanding wireless risks makes the idea of “secure control links” concrete, not just a checkbox.

Week 29 – Threat Modeling

Students create a threat model for their own drone system, identifying assets, possible attackers, and realistic attack paths.

Goals

  • Identify what needs protection (devices, data, people).
  • List potential attackers and their capabilities.
  • Draw attack paths that show how an incident could happen.

In-Class Activities

  • Fill out a structured threat-modeling worksheet for their drone.
  • Diagram data flows between controller, drone, and any external systems.
  • Share and compare threat models in small groups.

Why This Matters

Threat modeling is a systematic way to think about risk. Students practice structured reasoning instead of just listing random “bad things.”

Week 30 – Implementing Countermeasures

Based on their threat models, students choose and apply specific countermeasures to harden their drone’s communication and operation.

Goals

  • Connect threats to specific, realistic mitigations.
  • Implement at least one technical or procedural control.
  • Evaluate how well the chosen countermeasures address the risks.

In-Class Activities

  • Choose mitigations such as stronger pairing procedures, access control, or safe-flight rules.
  • Implement configuration changes or simple code-level protections where appropriate.
  • Test and document how the changes affect usage and security.

Why This Matters

Security is only real when it changes behavior or technology. Students learn that even small mitigations can significantly reduce risk.

Week 31 – Demonstration & Reflection

In the final week, students present their drones and explain not just how they fly, but how they addressed security and safety in their designs.

Goals

  • Showcase the completed drone and its capabilities.
  • Communicate design and security decisions clearly.
  • Reflect on what worked, what failed, and what they’d change.

In-Class Activities

  • Run final demonstration flights or bench demos as appropriate.
  • Have each group present their threat model and mitigations.
  • Facilitate a class discussion about lessons learned and next steps.

Why This Matters

The demo week ties the entire course together, celebrating student work and reinforcing both engineering and cybersecurity mindsets.
