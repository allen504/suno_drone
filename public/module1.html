Module 1: Designing & Modeling

In this module, students learn how to use CAD tools to design a lightweight drone frame optimized for strength, stability, and real-world fabrication with 3D printing.

Build Guide

Use the interactive guide below as a reference while you work through the weekly lessons.

Week 10 – Introduction to 3D Modeling

Students are introduced to CAD (Computer-Aided Design) software and learn how to create basic 3D shapes that will eventually become the drone frame.

Goals

  • Learn the basics of a CAD tool (e.g., Tinkercad, Onshape, or Fusion 360).
  • Understand how to create, move, rotate, and scale 3D shapes.
  • Develop spatial reasoning by viewing parts from multiple angles.

In-Class Activities

  • Instructor demo: navigating the CAD interface and using basic tools.
  • Students recreate a simple block-style drone frame from a reference.
  • Short challenges: “make a frame that is longer,” “add a camera mount cube,” etc.

Why This Matters

Students build confidence with digital design before working on more complex engineering constraints. This is the foundation for turning ideas into printable drone parts.

Week 11 – 3D Frame Design Constraints

Students learn that drone frames must balance weight, strength, and geometry. They begin designing their own frame with real engineering constraints.

Goals

  • Understand how weight, strength, and rigidity affect flight.
  • Consider motor spacing, propeller diameter, and center of gravity.
  • Plan mounting points for electronics, battery, and camera.

In-Class Activities

  • Analyze example drone frames and discuss trade-offs (light vs. strong).
  • Sketch a top-down view of the drone frame with labeled dimensions.
  • Students update their CAD models to respect motor spacing and center-of-gravity ideas.

Why This Matters

Real drones fail when design constraints are ignored. Students begin thinking like engineers, making intentional choices instead of random shapes.

Week 12 – CAD & Slicing for 3D Printing

Students take their digital frame from CAD into a slicer so it can be printed. They learn how slicer settings affect print strength, time, and quality.

Goals

  • Finalize a printable version of the drone frame in CAD.
  • Export the frame as an STL (or similar) file.
  • Understand key slicer settings: layer height, infill, supports, and print time.

In-Class Activities

  • Students clean up their CAD models (no floating parts, correct thicknesses).
  • Export STL files and open them in a slicer.
  • Experiment with slicer settings to see how they change estimated time and material usage.

Why This Matters

This week connects “on-screen” design to physical reality. Students learn that settings like infill and layer height can mean the difference between a fragile frame and a durable one.

Week 13 – 3D Printing the Drone Frame

Students 3D print their frames (or parts of them) and practice safe printer operation and basic troubleshooting.

Goals

  • Operate a 3D printer safely and responsibly.
  • Monitor a print and recognize common print failures.
  • Compare printed parts to the original CAD model.

In-Class Activities

  • Review printer safety: hot end, moving parts, ventilation, and supervision rules.
  • Start prints for student frames (staggered if there are limited printers).
  • Observe prints in progress and discuss issues like warping, poor adhesion, or stringing.

Why This Matters

Hands-on fabrication is a key maker skill. Students see how their design choices and slicer settings play out on a real machine.

Week 14 – Post-Processing & Assembly Prep

Students clean and refine their printed frames so they’re ready for electronics and motors. This includes sanding, drilling, and test-fitting key components.

Goals

  • Learn basic post-processing techniques for 3D printed parts.
  • Create clean mounting points for motors, battery, and electronics.
  • Verify that the frame is physically ready for later modules (electronics and assembly).

In-Class Activities

  • Clean up prints: remove supports, trim strings, lightly sand rough surfaces where needed.
  • Drill or ream mounting holes for motors and standoffs (with supervision and PPE).
  • Test-fit sample motors, battery, and control boards to confirm that everything fits on the frame.

Why This Matters

A design is only successful if it can be built. Post-processing teaches students how to bridge the gap from “just printed” to “ready for electronics,” setting up the next modules in the course.
