Module 2: Fabrication & 3D Printing

This module focuses on turning the drone frame from a digital model into a physical, flight-ready part. Students move from slicing to printing, post-processing, and preparing the frame for motors and electronics.

Main curriculum weeks: 12 (CAD + Slicing), 13 (3D Printing), 14 (Post-Processing & Assembly Prep), 15 (Review of Drone Electrical Architecture), 16 (Motor Driver Circuit)

Week 12 – CAD + Slicing

Students finalize their 3D drone frame models and learn how to slice them for printing, choosing settings that balance strength, print time, and material use.

Goals

  • Finalize a clean, printable 3D frame model.
  • Export the design as an STL (or similar) file.
  • Understand slicer basics: layer height, infill, supports, and print time.

In-Class Activities

  • Clean up CAD models (fix thin walls, remove floating geometry).
  • Export STL files and load them into a slicer.
  • Experiment with slicer settings and compare estimated print times and material usage.

Why This Matters

Students see how design decisions and slicer settings affect printability and durability, reinforcing the link between virtual design and real-world fabrication.

Week 13 – 3D Printing

With sliced models ready, students learn how to operate a 3D printer safely and supervise the printing of their drone frames or frame sections.

Goals

  • Safely operate a classroom 3D printer.
  • Monitor an active print and recognize common failures.
  • Compare the printed frame to the original model and expectations.

In-Class Activities

  • Review 3D printer safety (hot end, moving parts, ventilation, supervision).
  • Start prints for student frames and take turns monitoring progress.
  • Identify and discuss issues like warping, bed adhesion problems, and stringing.

Why This Matters

Students gain hands-on fabrication skills and learn to troubleshoot physical problems that software alone cannot solve.

Week 14 – Post-Processing & Assembly Prep

Students clean and refine their printed frames, making sure everything fits and the frame is robust enough for motors, props, and electronics.

Goals

  • Learn basic post-processing techniques for 3D printed parts.
  • Prepare precise mounting points for motors and hardware.
  • Verify that the frame is ready to accept electronics in later modules.

In-Class Activities

  • Remove supports, trim artifacts, and lightly sand or file rough edges.
  • Drill or ream mounting holes to match motor and standoff hardware.
  • Test-fit motors, battery, and control boards so students can spot issues early.

Why This Matters

A printed frame is not automatically ready for flight. Post-processing teaches students how to turn a rough print into a functional, reliable part.

Week 15 – Review of Drone Electrical Architecture

Students zoom out and look at the full electrical system of the drone: power, motors, sensors, radio, and control board, and how they all connect.

Goals

  • Map out all of the drone's electrical subsystems.
  • Understand how power flows from the battery to every major component.
  • Relate frame design decisions to where and how components will mount.

In-Class Activities

  • Draw a system-level diagram of the drone's wiring and components.
  • Discuss where each component will be physically mounted on the printed frame.
  • Identify potential cable routing and strain-relief strategies.

Why This Matters

Fabrication is not just printing a frame; it’s creating a structure that can cleanly host the entire electrical system without tangles or surprises later.

Week 16 – Motor Driver Circuit

Students build or assemble the motor driver stage that lets a low-power Arduino pin safely control high-current drone motors.

Goals

  • Understand why motor drivers are needed between the microcontroller and motors.
  • Wire or solder the motor driver circuit correctly.
  • Relate current, voltage, and power to real hardware limits.

In-Class Activities

  • Review example motor driver circuits and datasheets.
  • Assemble and wire the motor driver on perfboard or a small PCB.
  • Check for shorts, correct polarity, and neat, serviceable wiring.

Why This Matters

This week bridges fabrication and electronics. Students see how structural and electrical decisions work together to produce a safe, controllable drone.
